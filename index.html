Abstract
The German television advertisement market is dominated by complex market struc-
tures. As of today, no German television station is known to reduce the inherent in-
formation asymmetry typically found in private value markets by the introduction of
dynamic pricing mechanisms such as combinatorial auctions. Instead, stations rely on
enforced secrecy between bidders and multiple levels of discounts to achieve reasonable
revenues. After analyzing the market structure in general and domain speciﬁc auction
formats used or proposed in other countries for this market, this thesis elaborates a
new combinatorial auction format with a focus on eﬃciency and stability. Diﬀering
from a fully enumerative bidding language in which bidders would need to specify all
interested bundles explicitly, the presented language allows a bidder the express his
interests based on custom valuations such as coverage or demographic reach, closely
resembling eﬃciency metrics already known to media agencies. Finding optimal so-
lutions to the underlying allocation and pricing problems takes a prohibitively large
amount of time for realistic market sizes, but near-optimal solutions can be found
relatively quickly. In order to use these suboptimal allocations to generate reasonable
solutions, two generic methods for approximating core-selecting payments are devel-
oped and subsequently analyzed and compared in a series of numerical experiments.
Complementing the simulating environment, a distributed system is developed in or-
der to further explore and illustrate the usage of the presented bidding language in an
intuitive and graphical form.

2 The TV Advertisement Market
The television advertisement market is, with an annual turnover of 11.1 bn €, the
biggest advertisement market in the public sector, representing approximately 42% of
the annual amount invested in public advertising (see SevenOne Media, 2012b). While
it seems clear that this amount is a driving force in the television market, its impact
on each channel is not uniform.
According to Bauer (2001), German television channels can be divided into cat-
egories, depending which markets are targeted during airtime: The TV advertising
market consists of the television audience watching a broadcast with a buying in-
tent, such as an advertising broadcast. The TV recipients market, on the other hand,
consists of the audience watching a broadcast which (at least initially) does not demon-
strate a buying intent, but is watching because of the information or entertainment
provided by the channel. While some channels are either directed exclusively at the
former (such as QVC, a multinational corporation specializing in televised home shop-
ping), or at the latter (such as several government sponsored channels such as 3sat or
arte), the majority of channels is addressing both markets. If this is the case, inter-
dependencies between these markets cannot be avoided, as the success of a program
in the recipients market is directly inﬂuencing the amount of potential viewers of the
products or services displayed in the aired adverts (Bauer, 2001).
After the deregulation of frequencies for television channels starting in 1984, the
amount of channels a household can receive on average increased continuously, as seen
in ﬁg. 2.1. This increase in supply is not matched with a similar growth in demand,
however: Between 1985 and 1998 the average amount spent watching television in
Germany by an adult increased only about 37%. This market structure has caused

In order to diﬀerentiate itself from its competitors, channels typically try to provide

2.1 Market Structure – Channels, Agencies,
Campaigns
The creation and placement of an advert is typically not done by a brand owner
directly. Instead, the interested company approaches media agencies, which will be
then hired to create and/or get in contact with channels in order to air the ad. In order
to achieve an optimal response from the future audience, the creation and airing will
be tailored towards one or multiple target groups, i.e. a speciﬁc subset of the watching
population. A target group can be deﬁned by simple values such as the marriage
status, the age or sex of a viewer, but can also be arbitrarily complex, referring to

e.g. a speciﬁc social status or behavioral pattern. These groups represent the part of
the population typically seen as most susceptible audience regarding the ad, i.e. the
people most inclined to acquire the promoted product or service. In order to build up
network eﬀects and a higher level of brand awareness, adverts are typically not aired
once or sporadically, but grouped into a campaign. A campaign is directed at the
same target groups, takes place during a determined time span, and is measured by
its eﬃciency during and after airing. The two most prominent metrics in measuring
this eﬃciency are as follows:
CPGRP – Costs per Gross Rating Point
A Gross Rating Point (GRP) is deﬁned as the percentage of a speciﬁc target group
watching a single advert. A GRP of a campaign is then built by adding up all GRPs
of all single ads.
As an example, let’s suppose the target audience is male, and its age should be
between 25-55. Let’s further suppose the ad is aired ﬁve times, and during each airing
50% of the watchers matched the criteria of the target group. The GRP would then
be 250. The Costs per Gross Rating Point (CPGRP) is then deﬁned by dividing the
total campaign cost by the GRP achieved during airing. If 100 000 € was spent for
this campaign, the CPGRP would therefore be 400.
CPM – Costs per Mille
An alternative measure of eﬃciency is coined by the term Costs per Mille, or CPM.
Like the CPGRP metric, it is used as a benchmark for the relative cost of an adver-
tising campaign. In order to determine the CPM, the total number of viewers of all
relevant target groups are summed and divided through the total cost of the campaign.
Focusing on the same target group as in the previous paragraph, let us suppose that
each slot was watched by 1000 people. The CPM would then be calculated as follows:
CP M = 100 000/(5 · 0.5 · 1000) = 40.

Diﬀerence in measuring the campaign eﬃciency
As seen in the previous examples, the presented methods take an entirely diﬀerent
approach on how to measure eﬃciency: The CPM is a direct measurement of how
much was spent for each potentially interested viewer, and can be compared to other
traditionally used performance based tools such as the Costs per Acquisition metric

The concept of the CPGRP benchmark takes a very diﬀerent approach: The ab-
solute number of viewers is ignored, focusing on how many times an audience group
has seen an ad during the campaign, instead. Because of this, the CPGRP is only
useful as a metric to discover an ex-ante eﬃciency, usually used to determine on which
channel, or on which programs/time slots to air. The advertiser will still want to know
how many people were actually reached during and after a campaign run.

2.3 Drawbacks of the Fixed Price Approach
Even though the selection of advertisement time is greatly enhanced by the provided
ﬁltering methods, it is not possible for the buyer to discover or even approximate

3 Combinatorial Auctions
A solution concept that addresses the aforementioned pricing problem is the combina-
torial auction. In it, a number of items are auctioned concurrently, allowing bidders
to express and quantify their preferences of whole bundles of items. By auctioning
the provided set of items against the received bundle bids directly, the auctioneer
is able to allocate the provided resource more eﬃciently than by selling these items
separately. This gain in eﬃciency is directly achieved by the ability to express super-
or subadditivity between items (if items are complementarities or substitutes, respec-
tively), something not directly possible if the interested items were sold independently.
While the applications for combinatorial auctions are numerous, the perhaps most
prominent example are the “spectrum auctions”, as described in chapter 1. While
the market structure is not more complex than other procurement markets, the high
ﬁnancial stakes involved in these events has caused the adaption and reﬁnement of
several market designs for these auctions (see Cramton and Ausubel, 2006; Cramton,
2009).
In the following sections, two auction formats are presented and their implications
discussed. These formats will form the basis of the analysis of existing market designs
in the television advertisement market as seen in chapter 4 and in the developed market
design in chapter 5.

9 Conclusion
The structures used in the TV advertisement market did not change signiﬁcantly
over the last decades: The de-facto standard remains the acquisition of advertisement
time over a ﬁrst-come, ﬁrst-served pricing catalog. Additionally, the analysis of this
market regarding its eﬃciency and the creation of tailored market designs for it is
quite low. Speciﬁcally, no market design is known that is addressing the inherent
complementarities of the advertisement market and is also targeting eﬃciency and
stability, minimizing the incentive the deviate from the participants true valuations of
the requested items. This paper tries to remedy this situation, delivering a compact
bidding language for the market and combines it with a pricing rule that results in
total-payment minimizing bidder-Pareto optimal core pricing, a pricing rule that un-
der certain assumptions (see section 5.2) results in stable outcomes and incentivates
truthful behavior.
In order to cope with the computational expense required to solve the underlying
allocation and pricing problems, the presented allocation and pricing algorithms were
enhanced in their ability to be able to produce reasonable results also with suboptimal
solutions. This allows the presented market design to be used also for markets whose
size would be prohibitively large too solve in reasonable time, given the N P-hardness
of these problems. Two very diﬀerent approaches were created and subsequently ana-
lyzed regarding their overall revenue, speed, and incentive compatibility by running a
series of simulations for a realistically sized market. The results of these simulations
further supported the utility of the usage of suboptimal solutions for the tackled mar-
ket size, as it enabled the author to generate nearly optimal solutions and core prices.
Complementing the theoretical analysis and presentation of the said market de-

sign, a system was designed that enables interested parties to experience this market
“hands on”. The implemented prototype allows a bidder to express all relevant details
regarding his planned campaigns in an interactive and graphical way, allowing the par-
ticipation in said markets without the exposure to implementation details regarding
the underlying system structure and algorithms. Being built in a distributed manner
from the ground up, the system scales horizontally in all computationally expensive
components: By adding new computational units to the cluster, it is possible to clear
auctions in parallel without aﬀecting the overall performance of the system. This fa-

In order to further verify the utility of the presented design in this paper, simulations

act on behalf of their clients, the assumption of quasi-linear valuations regarding the
price of a campaign has yet to be proven. The exploration of further simulations
and alternative market designs that address also nonlinear valuations would therefore
provide an interesting series of topics to follow up upon.
